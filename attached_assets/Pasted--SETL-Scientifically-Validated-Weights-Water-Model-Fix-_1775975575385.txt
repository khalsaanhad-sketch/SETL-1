## SCIENTIFIC RESEARCH FINDINGS BEFORE THE CODE

The following weight changes are grounded in specific peer-reviewed papers and 
authoritative aviation regulatory documents. Here is the exact evidence base:

### LAND WEIGHTS — What research says

**Current SETL:** slope=0.38, roughness=0.22, distance=0.18, crowd=0.14, obstacle=0.08

**Slope (0.38 → 0.35)**  
Evidence: Penn State GIS HLZ study — expert AHP surveys consistently rank slope #1.  
STANAG 2999 (NATO) — slope is the primary site suitability criterion.  
ArcGIS/ESRI HLZ Solution — "Three primary factors: land cover, ground slope, and obstacles" (slope listed first).  
MDPI Remote Sensing 2021 helicopter landing study — slope threshold is the primary binary constraint.  
US Army ERDC GOAT report — slope is one of two primary quantitative criteria.  
Verdict: 0.35-0.40 is correct. Slight decrease to 0.35 makes room for the underweighted crowd criterion.

**Roughness (0.22 → unchanged)**  
Evidence: US Army ERDC GOAT (2021) — explicitly states "surface roughness is a KEY DISCRIMINATOR  
for site utility in complex terrain" for Army aviation LZ selection.  
Jin et al., Transactions in GIS (2025) — slope AND roughness are the two primary hazard metrics  
in probabilistic safe landing site selection.  
MDPI UAV emergency landing spot detection (2021) — roughness is second criterion after slope.  
Verdict: 0.22 is directly supported by multiple sources. Keep unchanged.

**Population density/crowd (0.14 → 0.20)**  
Evidence: NASA forced landing specifications (cited by Coombes et al., JAIS 2016) — three primary  
criteria are "risk to civilian population, reachability, and probability of safe landing"  
— population risk is listed FIRST.  
Di Donato & Atkins, AIAA Journal of Aerospace Information Systems — proposes using  
"real-time mobile phone activity to assist in avoiding densely occupied areas" — treating  
population as the primary ground risk metric.  
MDPI Drones 2025 gradient-guided search — "ground risk is quantified by employing discrete  
population census data" — population density IS the ground risk.  
Arxiv 2026 airspace-aware contingency landing — "ground risk is assessed from overflown  
population density" — direct support for higher crowd weight.  
Verdict: 0.14 underweights population risk relative to what all recent literature supports.  
Increase to 0.20.

**Distance (0.18 → 0.15)**  
Evidence: NASA forced landing specs — "reachability" is one of three primary criteria,  
but reachability in SETL is already handled by the GLIDE MASK (binary reachable/unreachable).  
Within the already-reachable cell set, distance is a secondary optimisation factor.  
ENAC automated landing selector (DASC 2022) — distance treated as secondary to terrain/occupancy.  
Verdict: Modest decrease from 0.18 to 0.15. The glide mask handles absolute reachability;  
within the reachable set, distance optimisation is secondary.

**Obstacle (0.08 → unchanged)**  
Evidence: ArcGIS HLZ Solution — obstacles ranked THIRD after slope and land cover.  
Penn State HLZ — obstacles included in AHP pairwise comparison but consistently assigned  
lower weight than slope and roughness.  
ICAO obstacle risk classification — aviation obstacles are important but modelled separately  
from terrain suitability (which SETL does correctly via the NOTAM engine).  
Verdict: 0.08 is appropriate given that SETL's obstacle engine only captures tower/mast/power  
obstacles from OSM and the NOTAM engine handles known runway/airport obstacles separately.

### WATER MODEL — What research says (and two critical bugs found)

**Current SETL water factors:** [distance=0.35, wind=0.30, crowd=0.20, slope=0.15]

**FAA AIM Section 6-3-3 (the definitive authoritative source):**
"A successful aircraft ditching is dependent on three primary factors. In order of importance:
1. Sea conditions and wind.  2. Type of aircraft.  3. Skill and technique of pilot."

**AOPA on ditching:** Fatality rate ~20% — highest of any emergency landing type.
"Hypothermia, the reduction of body temperature, claims nearly half the victims of ditchings."
This means proximity to shore (shallower water, faster rescue) is a critical survival factor.

**CRITICAL BUG 1 — slope is used backwards for water:**
The terrain engine assigns water cells these slope values:
  deep_ocean: slope_deg = 0.3
  continental_shelf: slope_deg = 1.2
  coastal_water: slope_deg = 0.5

In the TOPSIS water model, ALL columns including slope are cost columns (lower = better).
This means deep ocean (slope=0.3) appears BETTER than coastal water (slope=0.5).
In reality, deep ocean is FAR WORSE for ditching: greater hypothermia risk, no rescue nearby.
The slope column is meaningless for water and its current use is BACKWARDS.

FIX: Replace the "slope" input for water cells with a proper "depth_risk" score
computed from elevation_m:
  elevation_m < -2000 (deep ocean): depth_risk = 1.0 (maximum risk)
  -2000 <= elevation_m < -200 (continental shelf): depth_risk = 0.6
  -200 <= elevation_m < 0 (coastal water): depth_risk = 0.2
  elevation_m >= 0 (shoreline/land interface): depth_risk = 0.0 (unreachable on water anyway)

**CRITICAL BUG 2 — crowd is wrong sign for water:**
For land cells, crowd is a COST — high population density = danger to people on the ground.
For water cells, crowd near-coastal = maritime infrastructure, ports, boats = RESCUE PROXIMITY.
High crowd near a water cell means it is near a populated coast, which means faster rescue.
The TOPSIS currently treats water crowd as a cost (same as land) — this is WRONG.

FIX: For water TOPSIS, crowd must be a BENEFIT column (removed from cost_cols),
so that cells near populated coasts score BETTER, not worse.

**Wind (0.30 → 0.40):**
FAA AIM explicitly states sea conditions and wind are the #1 factor for ditching success.
AOPA: "Once winds exceed 35 knots, swells and seas will likely be in the same direction."
Wind speed is the best proxy for sea state when swell data is unavailable.
Verdict: Increase wind weight from 0.30 to 0.40 to match FAA's "sea conditions and wind = #1".

**Distance (0.35 → 0.20):**
Distance from aircraft is already handled by the glide mask for water cells too.
The more important spatial factor for water is PROXIMITY TO SHORE, which is captured
by depth_risk (coastal water = low depth_risk = better = closer to shore).
Verdict: Decrease distance weight; depth_risk now carries the proximity-to-rescue signal.

**Crowd (0.20 → 0.10, and FLIP to benefit):**
Reduce weight (maritime rescue proxy is less reliable than direct depth info)
but flip from cost to benefit (see bug fix above).

**Depth_risk (was slope 0.15 → 0.30):**
Research basis: AOPA — "hypothermia claims nearly half the victims of ditchings"
→ depth determines rescue time → coastal (low depth_risk) = survival advantage.
FAA AIM — "If possible, aim for shallow water" is the primary spatial guidance.
Verdict: Increase from 0.15 to 0.30 to reflect that shallow coastal water dramatically
improves survival compared to open ocean.

**UPDATED WATER WEIGHTS:** wind=0.40, depth_risk=0.30, distance=0.20, crowd=0.10
**crowd must be a BENEFIT (not in cost_cols)**

## STEP 24 — Update the absolute safety floor thresholds in `decision_engine.py`

The current absolute floor uses slope < 5.0° for the "good LZ" threshold.
This is correct per FAA helicopter class 1 performance (max 5°) and STANAG 2999.

However, the slope threshold for the `surface_score` calculation in `generate_cells()`
currently uses 15° as the maximum. Research supports this:
  - STANAG 2999: max 7° for most helicopters, 12-15° for some fixed-wing off-airport landing
  - NWCG wildfire helicopters: max 7°
  - For fixed-wing emergency landing, 15° is the practical upper limit before rollover risk

The current 15° threshold is acceptable for fixed-wing GA aircraft. Keep it unchanged.

Verify the absolute floor function is unchanged:
```python
def _apply_land_floor(prob: float, slope: float, roughness: float,
                      crowd: float, obstacle: float) -> float:
    # Very flat, clear terrain (runway/airstrip quality) → green floor
    if slope < 2.0 and roughness < 3.0 and crowd < 0.15 and obstacle < 0.15:
        return max(prob, 0.76)
    # Flat, open terrain (good emergency LZ) → at worst amber
    if slope < 5.0 and roughness < 8.0 and crowd < 0.35 and obstacle < 0.30:
        return max(prob, 0.55)
    return prob
```

The 2.0° runway-quality threshold is supported by FAA runway grade specs (<2% = <1.15°; 2° is slightly above runway grade but still very flat for off-airport LZ).
The 5.0° "good LZ" threshold directly matches the FAA helicopter class 1 max slope. ✓

No change needed here — these thresholds are well-validated.
